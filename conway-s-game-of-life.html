Conway's Game of Life

This article was last updated: 8 months ago

Conway Game of Life, a game about life. It was first proposed by the British mathematician John Horton Conway in 1970 as a cellular automaton. The so-called cellular automata can also be understood as a zero-player game. It is not like the “normal” game, which it does not requires too much operation. When we enter a certain initial value, the Conway Game of Life will run in accordance with the established rules and will not accept any parameters until the end of the game (unfortunately, you cannot win in the Conway Game of Life). When the game starts to run, we only need to observe how this life works, just like when we were child and we were quietly watching ants on the steps.

In Conway’s Game of Life, everything has a meaning, because it has the Turing completeness. In other words, any problems that may be solved with computers can be solved with Conway’s Game of Life. This was first proved by Bill Gosper in 1970. Although it rarely appears in our lives but they are still equally significant. In the game of life, we can also explore the real situation of life closer.

Rules of life

In this world live life, in a two-dimensional rectangular world. life has only two state, they can only be live (yellow) or dead (gray). The life and death of a life at the next moment depends on the number of lifes alive or dead in the eight adjacent squares.

  1. Life is afraid of loneliness
    If there are less than two lives around a life, it will die at the end of the round
       
  2. Life hates crowding
    If there are more than three lives around a life, it will also die at the end of the round
       
  3. Life can reproduce
    If there are three lives around the dead grid, it gains life at the end of the round
       
  4. Life like lagom
    If there are 2 or 3 lives around a life, it will stay the same at the end of the round
       

It is not difficult to find that the rules of Conway’s Game of Life are as familiar as the rules of our lives in this world.

Can life reproduce indefinitely?

From this point of view, it seems that a finite life cannot produce an infinite number of lives. This is also a conjecture that Conway started with this game:

Conway originally conjectured that no pattern can grow indefinitely—i.e. that for any initial configuration with a finite number of living cells, the population cannot grow beyond some finite upper limit

But this problem was quickly resolved. The answer to this answer is “Yes, life can reproduce indefinitely”.
